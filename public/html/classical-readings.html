Warren Buffett's Partnership Letters

1957 to 1970

Before he became synonymous with Berkshire Hathaway, Warren Buffett embarked on an investment journey that began with the establishment of the Buffett Partnership Limited in 1956.

Initially, this endeavor involved seven close friends and relatives who became limited partners in his venture. As Buffett's investment prowess became increasingly evident, he expanded his circle of partners by creating additional partnerships following the same model. These partnerships marked the early stages of his remarkable career as an investor and financial sage.

During the critical period between 1959 and 1969, Warren Buffett maintained a tradition of regularly sending letters to his partners. These letters served as a means of updating them on the progress and performance of their investments. They provide a unique window into Buffett's investment philosophy, strategies, and the wisdom that would ultimately shape his future success. These correspondences represent a valuable historical record of the early days of one of the most celebrated and influential investors of our time.

Warren Buffett's Unpublished Letters to Shareholders

1969 to 1976

Commencing in approximately 1969, Warren Buffett initiated an enduring tradition of crafting annual letters addressed to the shareholders of Berkshire Hathaway.

Encapsulating a wealth of timeless insights and pearls of investment wisdom, these letters have become an invaluable resource for both seasoned investors and newcomers to the world of finance.

Remarkably, a series of these epistles, spanning from 1969 to 1976, has not been officially published on Berkshire Hathaway's website. Consequently, these particular letters have acquired a somewhat colloquial moniker, being affectionately referred to as the "unpublished" letters to shareholders. This unique collection serves as a treasure trove of Buffett's early reflections and provides an exclusive glimpse into the formative years of one of the world's most iconic investors, making them a compelling and sought-after artifact in the realm of financial literature.

Berkshire Hathaway's Owner's Manual

1999

Warren Buffett, often regarded as the greatest investor of all time, has always been transparent about his investment philosophy and strategies. To provide shareholders with a comprehensive understanding of how Berkshire Hathaway operates and what they can expect as owners, Buffett penned what he affectionately calls the "Owner's Manual."

This document, included in the company's annual reports, serves as a guiding compass for Berkshire Hathaway shareholders. In this article, we'll delve into the key elements of Berkshire Hathaway's Owner's Manual and what it reveals about Warren Buffett's approach to investing.

The Berkshire Culture

At the heart of Berkshire Hathaway's Owner's Manual lies the importance of the company's culture. Buffett emphasizes the unique culture that drives Berkshire's success. He emphasizes the value of integrity, honesty, and the long-term perspective in all business dealings. Berkshire's decentralized structure allows its subsidiaries to operate independently while adhering to these core values.

Focus on Long-Term Ownership

Warren Buffett's investment horizon has always been long-term, and he encourages Berkshire Hathaway shareholders to adopt the same perspective. The Owner's Manual highlights the importance of owning shares in a business for the long haul, discouraging short-term speculation. Buffett believes that long-term ownership aligns interests and maximizes shareholder value.

Intrinsic Business Value

Buffett's emphasis on intrinsic business value is a central theme in the Owner's Manual. He encourages shareholders to focus on the economic realities of the businesses they own rather than short-term market fluctuations. Berkshire Hathaway invests in companies with strong fundamentals, competitive advantages, and predictable cash flows.

Rational Decision-Making

The Owner's Manual emphasizes rational decision-making when it comes to investing. Buffett advises shareholders to avoid making investment decisions based on emotions or market sentiment. Instead, he suggests a thoughtful and analytical approach to assessing potential investments, focusing on businesses with durable competitive advantages.

Berkshire's Principles of Risk Management

Buffett discusses risk management extensively in the Owner's Manual. He acknowledges that Berkshire Hathaway faces a variety of risks, from insurance liabilities to investment risks. Buffett's conservative approach to risk management involves setting aside significant reserves to cover potential losses, ensuring Berkshire remains financially strong even in challenging times.

The Role of Shareholders

Buffett recognizes the importance of informed and engaged shareholders. He encourages shareholders to read and understand the Owner's Manual, to vote responsibly on corporate matters, and to consider themselves as owners of the business, not just stockholders. Berkshire Hathaway's commitment to transparency underscores its respect for shareholders.

In conclusion, Berkshire Hathaway's Owner's Manual serves as a testament to Warren Buffett's commitment to transparency and his belief in the principles of long-term value investing. It offers valuable insights into the culture, decision-making process, and risk management strategies of one of the world's most successful conglomerates. For shareholders and investors, it provides a roadmap to understanding the core principles that have guided Berkshire Hathaway to unparalleled success under Buffett's leadership.

The Superinvestors of Graham and Doddsville

1984

Warren Buffett's "The Superinvestors of Graham and Doddsville" is a timeless essay that shines a light on the principles of value investing championed by Benjamin Graham and David Dodd, the forefathers of this investment philosophy.

Published in 1984, this seminal work continues to resonate with investors around the world, offering profound insights into the methods and mindset of successful value investors.

The essay begins by debunking the myth that stock market success is driven by the "efficient market hypothesis," which posits that all available information is reflected in stock prices, making it impossible for investors to consistently outperform the market. Buffett provides a compelling counterargument by showcasing the remarkable performance of a group of investors, all of whom were followers of Graham and Dodd's value investing principles.

Intrinsic Value and Fundamental Analysis

One of the central tenets of Graham and Dodd's approach is the concept of "intrinsic value" – the true worth of a stock, independent of its market price. Buffett highlights that the investors profiled in his essay all share this fundamental belief in determining intrinsic value through meticulous analysis of a company's financials and industry dynamics.

The Superinvestors

The essay introduces readers to the superinvestors themselves, including notable names like Buffett himself, Charlie Munger, Walter Schloss, Tom Knapp, Tom Phelps, Bill Ruane, Rick Guerin, Stan Perlmeter, Jack Byrne and Rick Rickertsen. Buffett emphasizes that these individuals hail from diverse backgrounds, have varying investment strategies, and employ different methods for valuation. However, what unites them is their commitment to the value investing principles espoused by Graham and Dodd.

Investment Success Stories

Buffett goes on to dissect some of the successful investments made by these superinvestors, showcasing instances where they identified undervalued stocks that later yielded substantial profits. The examples serve as compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the Graham and Dodd approach and the ability of these investors to consistently beat the market.

In conclusion, "The Superinvestors of Graham and Doddsville" serves as both a tribute to the enduring wisdom of Benjamin Graham and David Dodd and a testament to the fact that value investing remains a potent and profitable investment strategy. The essay reinforces the idea that, despite the prevailing belief in efficient markets, diligent analysis, patience, and a commitment to intrinsic value can empower investors to achieve extraordinary results over the long term. Warren Buffett's timeless work continues to inspire and educate aspiring investors, carrying forward the legacy of Graham and Dodd's value investing principles for generations to come.

Graham's insights and strategies provided a profound impact, serving as a catalyst for immediate implementation by those who recognized the immense value of his teachings. As these professionals began to apply Graham's principles and recommendations to their investment endeavors, they reaped tangible financial benefits, further underscoring the enduring relevance and effectiveness of Graham's approach to security analysis.

In the annals of investment education, Benjamin Graham's lectures stand as a testament to his exceptional ability to distill complex financial concepts into comprehensible and actionable insights. The resonance and applicability of his teachings, exemplified by the swift adoption by investment professionals, underscore the enduring relevance of Graham's wisdom and the profound impact he had on the evolution of investment strategies and financial education. These lectures continue to serve as a source of inspiration and knowledge for aspiring investors and financial professionals alike, illuminating the path to sound investment practices and enduring financial success.

Graham-Newman Partnership Letters

1946 to 1958

During his tenure as Benjamin Graham's protégé at Graham-Newman, Warren Buffett had the unique opportunity to observe firsthand the investment strategies and principles of his revered mentor. Much like Buffett, Graham also composed annual letters to his investment partners, providing insights into the firm's activities and investment approach.

However, in stark contrast to Buffett's later style, Graham's letters were characterized by their matter-of-fact, no-nonsense approach. Unlike the verbose and philosophical tone that would come to define Buffett's letters, Graham's writings were notably devoid of sermonizing or the delivery of pearls of investment wisdom to the reader. Instead, Graham's focus was primarily on the factual aspects of the partnership's operations and investments.

While Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett both made significant contributions to the world of value investing, their communication styles in their annual letters to partners were distinctly different. Graham's letters, marked by their straightforward and pragmatic tone, provided investors with a clear and concise account of the partnership's activities and financial performance.

In contrast, Buffett's letters would evolve over the years to encompass not only financial reporting but also extensive commentary on investment philosophy and life lessons, becoming a rich source of wisdom for generations of investors. These differing approaches in their letters reflect the unique personalities and communication styles of two of the most influential figures in the field of value investing.
