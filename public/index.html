About Us

WHO WE ARE

What is Graham-and-Doddsville?

Graham and Doddsville is a website dedicated to helping everyone to become better investors.

Our aim is to provide every investor from all walks of life with the right investment philosophy, strategies, tools and resources necessary to manage their own investment decisions.

Here you will learn to better analyse and evaluate companies (or their stocks), particularly those listed in the Australian stockmarket.

Where did our name come from?

The rather unusual name for the website is derived from the famous speech (later an article) entitled "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville" given by Warren Buffett on 17 May 1984 at the Columbia University School of Business.

Honouring the 50th anniversary of Benjamin Graham and David Dodd's highly influential book, Security Analysis, Buffett launched an attack on the Modern Portfolio Theory and the Efficient Market Hypothesis by examining the consistent market outperformance of a select group of investors, all of whom 'coincidentally' studied under the same teacher, namely, Graham.

Buffett dismissed the suggestion that these investors were merely the end product of something akin to a coin-flipping contest – a supposed game of pure chance in the field of stockmarket investing:

"I think you will find that a disproportionate number of successful coin-flippers in the investment world came from a very small intellectual village that could be called Graham-and-Doddsville. A concentration of winners that simply cannot be explained by chance can be traced to this particular intellectual village."

Buffett highlighted the 'common intellectual theme' of the investors of Graham-and-Doddsville as simply being the search for discrepancies between the value of a business and the price of small pieces of that business in the stockmarket. He also adds:

"If it doesn't make any difference whether all of a business is being bought on a Monday or a Friday, I am baffled why academicians invest extensive time and effort to see whether it makes a difference when buying small pieces of those same businesses. Our Graham & Dodd investors, needless to say, do not discuss beta, the capital asset pricing model, or covariance in returns among securities. These are not subjects of any interest to them. In fact, most of them would have difficulty defining those terms. The investors simply focus on two variables: price and value."

The original 'superinvestors' of Graham-and-Doddsville discussed in the speech / article were Walter Schloss, Tom Knapp & Ed Anderson, Bill Ruane, Charlie Munger, Rick Guerin, Stan Perlmeter, as well as Buffett himself.

Graham and Doddsville website aims to be the online home of value investing around the world.

  • Providing comprehensive and useful investing guides, research tools and other investment-related information for investors.

Buffett's emphasis on intrinsic business value is a central theme in the Owner's Manual. The Superinvestors of Graham and Doddsville

1984

Warren Buffett's "The Superinvestors of Graham and Doddsville" is a timeless essay that shines a light on the principles of value investing championed by Benjamin Graham and David Dodd, the forefathers of this investment philosophy.

Published in 1984, this seminal work continues to resonate with investors around the world, offering profound insights into the methods and mindset of successful value investors.

The essay begins by debunking the myth that stock market success is driven by the "efficient market hypothesis," which posits that all available information is reflected in stock prices, making it impossible for investors to consistently outperform the market. Buffett provides a compelling counterargument by showcasing the remarkable performance of a group of investors, all of whom were followers of Graham and Dodd's value investing principles.

Intrinsic Value and Fundamental Analysis

One of the central tenets of Graham and Dodd's approach is the concept of "intrinsic value" – the true worth of a stock, independent of its market price. Buffett highlights that the investors profiled in his essay all share this fundamental belief in determining intrinsic value through meticulous analysis of a company's financials and industry dynamics.

The Superinvestors

The essay introduces readers to the superinvestors themselves, including notable names like Buffett himself, Charlie Munger, Walter Schloss, Tom Knapp, Tom Phelps, Bill Ruane, Rick Guerin, Stan Perlmeter, Jack Byrne and Rick Rickertsen. Buffett emphasizes that these individuals hail from diverse backgrounds, have varying investment strategies, and employ different methods for valuation. However, what unites them is their commitment to the value investing principles espoused by Graham and Dodd.

Investment Success Stories

Buffett goes on to dissect some of the successful investments made by these superinvestors, showcasing instances where they identified undervalued stocks that later yielded substantial profits. The examples serve as compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the Graham and Dodd approach and the ability of these investors to consistently beat the market.

In conclusion, "The Superinvestors of Graham and Doddsville" serves as both a tribute to the enduring wisdom of Benjamin Graham and David Dodd and a testament to the fact that value investing remains a potent and profitable investment strategy. The essay reinforces the idea that, despite the prevailing belief in efficient markets, diligent analysis, patience, and a commitment to intrinsic value can empower investors to achieve extraordinary results over the long term. Warren Buffett's timeless work continues to inspire and educate aspiring investors, carrying forward the legacy of Graham and Dodd's value investing principles for generations to come.

Graham-Newman Partnership Letters

1946 to 1958

During his tenure as Benjamin Graham's protégé at Graham-Newman, Warren Buffett had the unique opportunity to observe firsthand the investment strategies and principles of his revered mentor. Much like Buffett, Graham also composed annual letters to his investment partners, providing insights into the firm's activities and investment approach.

However, in stark contrast to Buffett's later style, Graham's letters were characterized by their matter-of-fact, no-nonsense approach. Unlike the verbose and philosophical tone that would come to define Buffett's letters, Graham's writings were notably devoid of sermonizing or the delivery of pearls of investment wisdom to the reader. Instead, Graham's focus was primarily on the factual aspects of the partnership's operations and investments.

While Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett both made significant contributions to the world of value investing, their communication styles in their annual letters to partners were distinctly different. Graham's letters, marked by their straightforward and pragmatic tone, provided investors with a clear and concise account of the partnership's activities and financial performance.

In contrast, Buffett's letters would evolve over the years to encompass not only financial reporting but also extensive commentary on investment philosophy and life lessons, becoming a rich source of wisdom for generations of investors. These differing approaches in their letters reflect the unique personalities and communication styles of two of the most influential figures in the field of value investing.

Where did our name come from?

The rather unusual name for the website is derived from the famous speech (later an article) entitled "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville" given by Warren Buffett on 17 May 1984 at the Columbia University School of Business.

Honouring the 50th anniversary of Benjamin Graham and David Dodd's highly influential book, Security Analysis, Buffett launched an attack on the Modern Portfolio Theory and the Efficient Market Hypothesis by examining the consistent market outperformance of a select group of investors, all of whom 'coincidentally' studied under the same teacher, namely, Graham.

Buffett dismissed the suggestion that these investors were merely the end product of something akin to a coin-flipping contest – a supposed game of pure chance in the field of stockmarket investing:

"I think you will find that a disproportionate number of successful coin-flippers in the investment world came from a very small intellectual village that could be called Graham-and-Doddsville. A concentration of winners that simply cannot be explained by chance can be traced to this particular intellectual village."

Buffett highlighted the 'common intellectual theme' of the investors of Graham-and-Doddsville as simply being the search for discrepancies between the value of a business and the price of small pieces of that business in the stockmarket. He also adds:

"If it doesn't make any difference whether all of a business is being bought on a Monday or a Friday, I am baffled why academicians invest extensive time and effort to see whether it makes a difference when buying small pieces of those same businesses. Our Graham & Dodd investors, needless to say, do not discuss beta, the capital asset pricing model, or covariance in returns among securities. These are not subjects of any interest to them. In fact, most of them would have difficulty defining those terms. The investors simply focus on two variables: price and value."

The original 'superinvestors' of Graham-and-Doddsville discussed in the speech / article were Walter Schloss, Tom Knapp & Ed Anderson, Bill Ruane, Charlie Munger, Rick Guerin, Stan Perlmeter, as well as Buffett himself.

MARKET QUOTES

"The market is a voting machine in the short run, but a weighing machine in the long run."

This famous quote from Benjamin Graham emphasizes the difference between short-term market sentiment and long-term fundamental value. In the short run, stock prices are driven by investor emotions, speculation, and market sentiment (the "voting machine"). However, over the long term, stock prices reflect the true underlying value of companies based on their earnings, assets, and growth prospects (the "weighing machine").

Benjamin Graham The Intelligent Investor

"Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."

Warren Buffett's simple yet profound statement about the fundamental principle of value investing. The price you pay for a stock should always be less than the intrinsic value you receive. This principle guides investors to focus on finding undervalued companies rather than chasing expensive stocks based on market trends or speculation.

Warren Buffett Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting

"Rule No. 1: Never lose money. Rule No. 2: Never forget rule No. 1."

Buffett's famous rules emphasize the importance of capital preservation in investing. While it's impossible to never lose money in the short term, the principle is to focus on investments with a high probability of success and to avoid speculative investments that could result in permanent capital loss. This conservative approach has been central to Berkshire Hathaway's long-term success.

Warren Buffett Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting

"The stock market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient."

This quote highlights the importance of patience in investing. Impatient investors often buy high and sell low, driven by fear and greed. Patient investors, on the other hand, can take advantage of market volatility to buy quality companies at attractive prices and hold them for the long term, allowing compound growth to work in their favor.

Warren Buffett Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting

"Be fearful when others are greedy and greedy when others are fearful."

This contrarian investment philosophy suggests that the best investment opportunities often arise when the market is in a state of panic or excessive optimism. When others are fearful and selling, it may be time to buy quality assets at discounted prices. Conversely, when everyone is greedy and buying, it may be time to be cautious and consider taking profits.

Warren Buffett Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting

"It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price."

This principle emphasizes the importance of business quality over price. A wonderful company with strong competitive advantages, excellent management, and predictable earnings can create significant value over time, even if purchased at a fair (not deeply discounted) price. A mediocre company, even at a cheap price, may not provide the same long-term returns.
